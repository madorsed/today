Pat Kenny gave up the fight to stop apartment blocks construction

Edmund Scott| January 3, 2020| 0 Comments

Broadcaster Pat Kenny gave up his fight to stop the construction of apartment blocks on lands next to his home in Dalkey.

The host of Newstalk said he was “absolutely behind” an effort by the High Court to block the same developer from constructing a mixed-use development at Bulloch Harbour in another part of Dalkey. In the case of the decision on the An Bord Pleanála Bulloch Harbour, citizens have already collected € 30,000 to finance the litigation for the High Court trial.

In a vote last July to give Richard Barrett’s Bartra consent for apartments on nearby land to the Kenny house, the planning board reversed a ruling by Dún Laoghaire Rathdown County Council.

Despite strong resistance from Mr. Kenny and his wife Kathy, along with other local residents, the board of appeals accepted the proposal. The Kennys and other objectors had the right in the courts to contest the ruling of the appeals board.

Mr. Kenny said: “The deadline has passed for judicial review. Judicial review is extremely expensive and even if you win there is no guarantee that you will be awarded your legal costs.

“And if you win, there is nothing to stop a developer going in again and tweaking things and forcing you through the whole process all over again with no certainty of success . . . All we can do now is insist that the council enforce the strict conditions laid down by An Bord Pleanála.”

In both Bartra cases, the planning committee overturned the decisions of the auditor and granted permission. However local people are going down the path of judicial review in a bid to prevent the other Bartra development process at Bulloch Harbour.

At the Killiney Castle Hotel, the Bulloch Harbor Heritage Association organized a public meeting attended by about 250 residents. District TD Richard Boyd Barrett and State Minister for Education Mary Mitchell O’Connor were also present along with Pat and Kathy Kenny.

Mr. Kenny also rejected An Bord Pleanála’s decision, which he described as “wrong-headed and must be avoided.” At the High Court in early October, the heritage association was given permission to challenge the planning board’s judgment and the case was postponed until December.
